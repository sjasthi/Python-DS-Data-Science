## How to Use This Guide

Read each question carefully, think about how it connects to your project, and practice answering out loud. Good answers are specific — always tie your response back to **your dataset and your findings**. It is perfectly fine to say "we didn't explore that, but here is what we did…"

---

## Category 1: Your Project & Problem Statement

**Q1. Can you walk us through what problem your project is solving and why it matters in the real world?**

> *What they're looking for:* A clear, confident explanation of your "why." Industry experts want to know you understand the purpose behind the data, not just the code.

---

**Q2. Where did your data come from, and why did you choose that particular dataset?**

> *What they're looking for:* Awareness of data sources — whether you used a CSV file, pulled from Kaggle, or scraped a website with Beautiful Soup. Bonus points for explaining what made the dataset interesting or challenging.

---

**Q3. What question were you trying to answer with your analysis? Did the data actually help you answer it?**

> *What they're looking for:* The ability to connect your analysis back to a central question. Be honest if the data raised more questions than it answered — that is a real data science outcome too.

---

**Q4. If you had to explain your project to a friend who has never taken a data science class, how would you describe it in two sentences?**

> *What they're looking for:* Communication skills. Experts value people who can simplify complex ideas without losing accuracy.

---

## Category 2: Data Collection & Preparation

**Q5. What format was your raw data in — CSV, Excel, JSON, or something else — and how did you load it into Python?**

> *What they're looking for:* Familiarity with pandas file I/O methods like `pd.read_csv()` or `pd.read_excel()`, and comfort discussing data formats.

---

**Q6. What was the messiest part of your dataset, and how did you clean it?**

> *What they're looking for:* Real experience with data cleansing — handling missing values, fixing data types, removing duplicates, or standardizing formats using pandas.

---

**Q7. Did your dataset have any missing values? How did you decide what to do with them?**

> *What they're looking for:* Knowledge of strategies like dropping rows, filling with the mean/median, or forward-filling — and the reasoning behind choosing one over another.

---

**Q8. Did you scrape any data from a website using Beautiful Soup? If so, walk us through how you identified the HTML elements you needed.**

> *What they're looking for:* Understanding of HTML structure (tags, classes, IDs) and how Beautiful Soup's `find()` and `find_all()` methods are used to extract specific content.

---

## Category 3: Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

**Q9. What did your data look like before you started analyzing it? What were the first things you checked?**

> *What they're looking for:* Use of EDA methods like `df.head()`, `df.info()`, `df.describe()`, and `df.shape` to understand the dataset's size, types, and basic statistics.

---

**Q10. What was the most surprising or unexpected thing you found in your data during exploration?**

> *What they're looking for:* Intellectual curiosity and the ability to notice anomalies, outliers, or patterns that weren't expected — a hallmark of good data analysis.

---

**Q11. Did you use GroupBy or pivot tables in your analysis? What insight did that give you?**

> *What they're looking for:* Practical use of `df.groupby()` or `df.pivot_table()` to aggregate and summarize data across categories, and the ability to explain what the results revealed.

---

**Q12. How did you decide which columns (features) were important and which ones were not useful for your analysis?**

> *What they're looking for:* Critical thinking about relevance — dropping columns with too many nulls, irrelevant identifiers, or redundant data.

---

## Category 4: Data Visualization

**Q13. Which visualization library did you use the most — matplotlib, seaborn, or plotly — and why did you choose it?**

> *What they're looking for:* Understanding of when to use each tool. Matplotlib for customization, seaborn for statistical visuals, plotly for interactivity.

---

**Q14. Walk us through one of your charts. What type of chart is it, and what insight does it communicate?**

> *What they're looking for:* The ability to explain a visual clearly — what is on each axis, what pattern it shows, and what conclusion it supports.

---

**Q15. Why did you choose that type of chart for that specific data? For example, why a bar chart instead of a pie chart, or a line chart instead of a scatter plot?**

> *What they're looking for:* Intentional chart selection. Experts notice when students understand that chart type should match the data relationship being shown.

---

**Q16. What makes a data visualization "good" in your opinion? Does your project's visualizations meet that standard?**

> *What they're looking for:* Self-awareness and design thinking — clear labels, appropriate colors, readable scales, and a title that states the insight, not just the data.

---

## Category 5: Python & Programming

**Q17. Can you show us a piece of Python code from your project that you are most proud of, and explain what it does?**

> *What they're looking for:* Confidence in reading and explaining code — not just writing it. Pick something that shows pandas, numpy, or visualization skills.

---

**Q18. Did you use any numpy arrays in your project? What advantage did numpy give you over a regular Python list?**

> *What they're looking for:* Understanding that numpy arrays support vectorized operations and are faster and more memory-efficient than lists for numerical data.

---

**Q19. Did you write any classes or functions to organize your code? Why is that a good practice?**

> *What they're looking for:* Application of object-oriented programming and the concept of reusability — topics from the early weeks of the course.

---

**Q20. If your code broke or gave you an unexpected result, how did you debug it?**

> *What they're looking for:* Problem-solving habits — using print statements, checking data types, reading error messages, and testing one step at a time.

---

## Category 6: Insights & Conclusions

**Q21. What is the single most important finding from your project? What should someone take away after watching your presentation?**

> *What they're looking for:* The ability to distill analysis into a clear, memorable conclusion. Industry experts call this "the headline."

---

**Q22. If a business or organization were looking at your findings, what decision could they make based on your analysis?**

> *What they're looking for:* Real-world applicability. Even student projects can point toward actionable recommendations — show you've thought about the "so what."

---

**Q23. What are the limitations of your analysis? What conclusions should NOT be drawn from your data?**

> *What they're looking for:* Intellectual honesty. Great data scientists know what their data cannot prove, not just what it can.

---

## Category 7: Reflection & Next Steps

**Q24. If you had more time, what would you add to this project? Is there additional data you would want to collect?**

> *What they're looking for:* Forward thinking and curiosity. This question also reveals whether you fully understand your own project's scope.

---

**Q25. What was the hardest part of this project, and what is one thing you would do differently if you started over?**

> *What they're looking for:* Maturity and self-reflection. Experts respect students who can identify their own mistakes and articulate what they learned from them.

---

## Quick Preparation Tips

| Tip | Why It Matters |
|-----|----------------|
| Know your dataset inside and out | Many questions will circle back to your specific data |
| Be ready to show your code | Have your notebook open and easy to navigate |
| Practice your "2-minute summary" | Experts may ask you to summarize quickly |
| It is okay to say "I don't know, but..." | Pair it with related knowledge you do have |
| Connect everything to your question | Every answer should tie back to your project's purpose |
